# You fix a typo in a card's question and reload the deck. Does its saved progress survive? Predict what a card's *identity* actually depends on.
	`id()` hashes the subject and the back (answer) lines.
	The front and the note are deliberately left out of the hash.
	So rewording the front keeps the same id — progress stays attached.
	% at: 152-156
	! Identity is the card's content (its answer), not how you happen to ask it.

# Two cards in *different* decks happen to share the exact same answer line. Same identity, or different? Predict why.
	The subject (the deck's file name) is hashed first, before the back lines.
	So the same answer in two decks hashes to two different ids.
	The deck name namespaces every card it contains.
	% at: 158-159
	! Move a card to another file and it reads as a new card — same text, new id.

# A cloze card hashes something other than its visible answer. Predict what it hashes, and why that's necessary.
	When `hash_lines` is `Some`, those lines are hashed instead of `back`.
	A cloze sub-card hashes the raw marked-up line plus its hole index.
	That keeps each hole's id stable across rewording, and unique per hole.
	% at: 54-57,160
	! Two holes with identical answer text still get distinct ids via the index.

# The comment insists id() "must stay stable across versions." Predict what actually breaks if the hash ever changes.
	The id is the key under which each card's progress is stored.
	Change the hash and every stored key stops matching its card.
	All existing progress would be orphaned — silently reset to new.
	% at: 154-156
	! That risk is why a test pins id() to a fixed number (`id_is_stable`).
