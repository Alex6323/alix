I · how learning works

Rereading feels like learning.
It mostly isn’t.

Read a chapter twice and it feels familiar. But familiarity is recognition — the ability to nod along. Understanding shows up as recall: producing the idea yourself, with the book closed.

Psychologists call the gap the illusion of fluency: rereading and highlighting rate high on effort and low on effect.

Ask the room: who rereads and highlights before an exam? That's most of us. The problem isn't laziness — it's that smooth reading feels like mastery. Recognition and recall are different memory operations, and exams (and life) demand recall.

Dunlosky's 2013 review rated rereading and highlighting among the least effective techniques — reference list is at the end of the deck.

I · how learning works

What works: retrieval.

Trying to pull something out of memory strengthens it more than putting it in again. Let’s do one:

flip card

What is the capital of Australia?

Canberra — not Sydney, not Melbourne.
That small struggle you just felt? That is the rehearsal that makes memory stick — the testing effect.

Pause on the question. Let the room actually guess — most people say Sydney. The moment of effortful retrieval (even a failed one, followed by the answer) beats another exposure to the fact.

Roediger & Karpicke 2006: a week later, students who had been tested remembered substantially more than students who restudied for the same time. That's why flashcards work at all — they force retrieval.

I · how learning works

…and spacing.

Memory decays on a curve. Each successful recall, made just before you’d forget, flattens it — so the reviews get rarer while the memory gets stronger.

That schedule is mechanical — exactly what software is for. Spaced-repetition systems have run it since the 1970s.

The dashed curve is Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve — first measured in 1885. The amber curve is what spaced reviews do: each retrieval resets retention and slows the next decay, so intervals stretch from hours to days to weeks.

Cepeda's 2006 meta-analysis (250+ studies): spacing reliably beats massed practice. Combining the two robust effects — retrieval + spacing — is the entire premise of spaced-repetition software.

I · how learning works

The new trap: AI answers feel like understanding.

A large randomized trial gave ~1,000 students GPT-4 for math practice. While the AI was there, practice went great. Then came an exam without it:

−17 %

— worse than students who never had AI at all. A guardrailed tutor that wouldn’t just hand over solutions eliminated the harm. But it added no gain.

Bastani et al., “Generative AI Can Harm Learning”, PNAS 2025. Randomized controlled trial, ~1,000 high-school students.

This is the strongest clean RCT in the space so far. The mechanism is the fluency illusion again, supercharged: the AI produces the answer, you nod along, and the feeling of understanding is even smoother than rereading — while the retrieval never happens.

Be fair to the result: the guardrailed tutor didn't hurt — it just didn't help either. Access to answers isn't the bottleneck of learning. Verification is.

Don’t use AI to get answers.
Use it to check you understand.

The thesis of the talk, and the design rule behind the tool in the second half. An AI that hands you answers competes with your learning. An AI that questions you, grades your explanation against the source, and refuses to wave you through — that one works for your learning.

II · alix

alix — a plain-text trainer that takes this seriously.

Spaced repetition at the core, an AI layer woven through — a tutor on any card, generated decks, and an exam that gates progress on verified understanding. In your browser — an adult view, or a simpler one for kids. Local-first. Open source.

Honest framing for the room: this is early software, built in the open by one developer with AI assistance — the “honestly” section near the end covers its limits. What's shown here is real and shipping today.

II · alix

Your cards are plain text you own.

# What does the CAP theorem state?
    A distributed store can guarantee only two of:
    Consistency, Availability, Partition tolerance.
    ! The trade-off only bites while a partition is happening.
    % reveal: line

A deck is a file. Write it in any editor, version it with git, grep it, keep it for decades. No account, no database, no lock-in — and one comment-style directive can change how a card quizzes you.

Walk the sample: # starts a question, the indented lines are the answer, ! is a note shown after answering, and % lines are directives — this one reveals the answer one line at a time, so an ordered idea is recalled step by step.

Plain text is a values statement: your knowledge shouldn't live in someone's database.

II · alix

Review, the classical part — done properly.

  • FSRS scheduling — the spacing curve from Act I, automated.
  • Three session depths you pick per sitting — recognize, recall, reconstruct — over flip, cloze, and line-by-line cards, with both-direction and typed checks where they fit.
  • New cards arrive as a low-stakes attempt first, never a cold quiz.

Nothing on this slide needs AI — the flashcard core runs completely standalone. The point of the modes: an ordered procedure drills line-by-line, exact syntax drills by typing, a concept drills by explaining. The test method should match the material.

II · alix

Stuck on a card? Ask.

One keypress opens a tutor on the card you’re looking at — grounded in the card, its deck, and (if you allow it) the deck’s actual source. Good explanations can be saved back onto the card as a note.

Note what this is not: an open chat window. The tutor serves the card in front of you — the AI never floats free of the learning loop. And it runs through a CLI you're already signed into; alix itself stores no API keys.

II · alix

Drilling proves recall.
The exam checks understanding.

A deck can name its source — a chapter, a paper, real code. Once the cards are drilled, the AI writes fresh open questions from that source and grades your written answers against it — never against your cards. Pass, and the topics that build on this one unlock.

This is the Bastani lesson as architecture: the AI's job is verification, not answer supply. Grading against the source (not the cards) matters — grading you on cards you wrote would be circular. Fail, and the gaps become new remediation cards; re-drill and re-sit.

II · alix

Traces — drill the edges, not just the facts.

Cards hold the nodes of what you know. A trace walks a real path through a source — every step you predict what happens next, then the actual lines are revealed, and you judge your gap.

  • predict → reveal → judge — the retrieval effect, applied to connections
  • the excerpt is read live from the source; the source stays the oracle
  • the AI can discover the path for you (alix generate --trace)

For a CS audience this is the feature to slow down on: following a request through a codebase, a proof through a paper. Understanding is mostly edges — how one step forces the next — and passive reading of code never builds them. Prediction does.

II · alix

Start from your material.

  • alix generate <url-or-path> — a facts deck from a lecture page, a chapter, a file, a repo — with citations back into the source.
  • alix generate <dir> --goal "…" — a whole learning workspace: decks and traces, ordered by prerequisites, from one goal.
  • Generated decks are a starting point — read them before you trust them.

The demo that lands best: take the actual course page or lecture notes the audience is studying right now, and generate a deck from it live. Own material beats canned examples.

Keep the honest caveat on the slide: AI-generated cards need a human read-through. alix treats them as drafts, not truth.

III · honestly

What alix is not — on purpose.

  • Not an answer machine — it won’t do tonight’s homework.
  • Not a chat wrapper — the AI never floats free of a card, a deck, a source.
  • Not gamified — no streaks, no XP, no leaderboards.
  • Not a cloud service — no accounts, nothing to sync, nothing that can shut down and take your decks with it.
  • Not a content marketplace — you bring or generate the material from your sources.

Every refusal buys the one thing it is:

verified understanding,
on a schedule,
in files you own.

Each refusal is a written-down design decision, and each has market evidence behind it: answer machines are the −17 % condition at mass scale; gamification is where the VC money went and where the user backlash lives; a cloud study startup died in 2026 and took its users’ decks with it; marketplaces optimize for content volume over learning.

If the audience remembers one slide, make it this one — the negative space is the clearest definition of the tool.

III · honestly

What you should know before trying it.

  • Early software, pre-1.0 — rough edges, and breaking changes still happen.
  • Browser-based and local only. No mobile apps yet, no sync service.
  • The AI features need an AI CLI installed and signed in — Claude by default; Gemini, Codex, and Copilot work too. That usually means a paid account you already have.
  • The flashcard core runs entirely without AI — offline, standalone.
  • Privacy: everything stays on your machine, except the AI calls you explicitly make through your own account.

Give this slide full airtime — it's what makes the rest of the talk credible. If someone in the room needs a polished, proven product today, Anki has two decades of maturity; recommend it honestly. alix is for people curious about the verified-understanding loop and comfortable with early tools.

III · honestly

Where it sits.

flashcard apps

  • retrieval + spacing, proven
  • recall of isolated facts
  • no check that ideas connected

AI chat tutors

  • explain anything, instantly
  • no schedule, no retention
  • answers on tap — the −17 % trap

alix tries the intersection

  • plain text, local, yours
  • spaced retrieval for memory
  • AI that verifies, not answers

Each neighbor does its own job well. The combination is the empty spot alix aims at — whether it fills it well is for you to judge.

Deliberately modest: Anki is excellent at what it does; ChatGPT is a genuinely useful explainer. The claim is only that nobody occupies the intersection — plaintext + local + spaced repetition + AI-verified understanding — and that the Bastani result suggests the intersection matters.

A · appendix — the exam, precisely

  • Questions are written fresh from the % source:, favoring application and connections over card facts.
  • Grading strictness is a property of the material: strict / balanced / lenient, set per deck.
  • Failing produces remediation cards from your actual gaps — re-drill, re-sit.
  • A deck turns exam due once every card graduates to FSRS’s review phase — no separate unlock knob to set.
  • Drilling is never locked — prerequisites gate only the exam.

The design intent: verification gates progression, but nothing ever stops you from practicing. Locks apply to claiming mastery, not to studying.

A · appendix — likely questions

Does my data leave my machine?No — decks and progress are local files. Only the AI calls you make go out, through your own CLI account.
Which AI do I need?Any one of: Claude Code (default), Gemini CLI, Codex CLI, Copilot CLI — signed in. Codex can’t fetch URLs; use local sources there.
Does it work offline?The whole flashcard core, yes. AI features need the network.
What does it cost?alix is free and open source. The AI backend uses a subscription you likely already have.
Can I import from Anki?Cards, yes (TSV export) — scheduling history, no.

The privacy answer matters most in school contexts: alix is a local binary, not a service — there is no account and no server-side anything.
