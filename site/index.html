Is alix for you?

Five situations it's built for, then an honest list of where it plainly isn't there yet.

You're working through a book, a course, or a codebase, and it needs to actually stick

→ Point alix at the source and it drafts a deck (or a connected set of decks) from it, then brings the cards back on a schedule instead of leaving you to re-read and hope.

An AI explained something to you, and privately you're not sure you could reproduce it without help

→ alix's exam asks you fresh questions about the material and grades your written answer against the source itself, not a multiple-choice key, before it marks the topic done.

You mostly want solid spaced repetition for plain facts, terms, or vocabulary

→ The core is a plain-text review engine (fill-in-the-blank cards, both-direction review, images) that works with no AI at all; the AI pieces are optional on top.

You're a parent or teacher building a set of cards for a kid to review

→ alix can serve a second, simpler screen aimed at kids: tap-to-answer, no keyboard. You build and check the deck yourself; the kid just opens it.

You already use Anki and wonder if there's a reason to look elsewhere

→ You can bring existing cards in with a plain import. What's different is the exam and the tutor layered on top, but if a mature tool with a large shared-deck community is what you need, Anki still does that better.

alix isn't (yet) for you if you want…

  • A mobile app you download from a store. alix runs as a local web app you start from a terminal; a phone or tablet browser works fine, but there's no app-store listing yet.
  • Zero setup for the AI parts. The tutor, deck generation, and the exam need you to separately install and log into an AI assistant (Claude Code by default, or Gemini, Codex, Copilot); that's extra setup, and typically its own subscription too.
  • Automatic sync across your devices. Everything is plain files, no server, no accounts. Keeping them in sync means putting the folder in your own cloud tool (Dropbox, iCloud, Syncthing); alix doesn't do it for you.
  • A finished, settled tool. alix is pre-1.0 and still changing: the deck format and the progress store can change between updates with no migration path, so an update can lose your review history.
  • A library of ready-made decks. alix is built around decks you generate or write from your own material, not a marketplace of existing content.

Understand, don't just remember

Most tools optimize recall of isolated facts. alix's AI exam grades you against the source and unlocks what's next only once you can actually explain it.

A tutor on every card

Stuck or curious? Ask, and the AI explains in context — grounded in your deck's own source. Runs on the AI CLI you're already signed into: Claude by default — Gemini, Codex, and Copilot work too.

Proven spaced repetition

FSRS scheduling over plain-text decks you own — cloze, dual-direction, images, and dependencies between topics.
